# The Science of Viral Content — Research Reference

A deep dive on what the academic and industry research actually says about why content goes viral, organized for @theheat editorial reference.

## How to Use This Document

**The order matters. We work top to bottom.**

If we're not chuckling at our own tweets, sending them to friends, and finding them genuinely astounding, none of the platform mechanics save us. **Get the content right first.** Then — and only then — use platform tactics to amplify what already deserves attention.

This document is organized in that priority order:

1. **PART 1 — CONTENT** (the only part that matters most days). Why people share, what makes them stop, how to write tweets that land.
2. **PART 2 — AUDIENCE PSYCHOLOGY** (deeper craft). What the research says about negativity, identity, moral outrage, climate-specific behavior.
3. **PART 3 — PROOF POINTS** (case studies). What's actually gone viral and why.
4. **PART 4 — ANTI-PATTERNS** (the kill list). What guarantees the tweet flops.
5. **PART 5 — AMPLIFICATION** (platform mechanics). Reference, not religion. Use these to amplify already-great content. Never use them to rescue mediocre content.

**Compiled April 2026 for @theheat editorial reference.**

## Executive Diagnosis

The bot's default output is informational/weather-report content. Peer-reviewed research is unambiguous: **informational content triggers low-arousal cognitive processing, which is the single most reliable predictor of NON-sharing.** The fix is not "more emotion" — it's engineering content that specifically activates high-arousal states (awe, anger, anxiety, amusement) while satisfying social currency needs.

**Sadness is the default emotional response to climate data. Sadness is specifically named in peer-reviewed research as the emotion that suppresses sharing.** The job is to find frames that activate without falling into despair.

# PART 1 — CONTENT

This is where 90% of the work lives. If a tweet doesn't make you stop and feel something, no platform trick saves it.

## 1. Why People Share — The Emotional Activators

### 1.1 Berger & Milkman 2012 — "What Makes Online Content Viral?"
*Journal of Marketing Research* 49(2): 192–205. The foundational empirical paper.

**Study:** Analyzed ~7,000 NYT articles over 3 months, tracking which hit the "most emailed" list. Controlled for author fame, timing, day of week, length, complexity, section, prominence.

**Findings:**
- **Positive content is more viral than negative content** (~13% bump, all else equal).
- **But arousal trumps valence.** The mechanism is physiological activation, not positivity.
- **High-arousal positive emotion (awe) → more viral.** A one SD increase in awe-evoking-ness raised probability of hitting "most emailed" by ~30%.
- **High-arousal negative emotions (anger, anxiety) → more viral.**
- **Low-arousal emotion (sadness, contentment) → LESS viral, even if valenced.**

### 1.2 Berger 2011 — Arousal Is Sufficient
*Psychological Science* 22(7): 891–893.

**The jogging experiment:** 40 undergrads. Group A sat still 60 seconds. Group B jogged in place. Both then read a neutral news article. **Joggers forwarded the article significantly more.**

**Implication:** Arousal is sufficient by itself to increase sharing — it doesn't need to be "about" the content. **Our job is to activate readers, not just inform them.**

### 1.3 Which Emotions Drive vs Kill Sharing

**DRIVERS (high-arousal):**
| Emotion | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Awe** | Most powerful. Self-transcendent. Scale-shock. |
| **Anger** | Highly viral. Yuan et al. 2024: aggressive climate messaging drives more retweets. Anger > anxiety on retweet cascade depth. |
| **Anxiety** | Viral, but elicits avoidance long-term (climate fatigue). |
| **Amusement** | High-arousal positive; extremely shareable. |
| **Excitement** | Signals novelty. |
| **Surprise** | Doesn't work alone but amplifies whatever emotion is attached. |

**KILLERS (low-arousal):**
| Emotion | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Sadness** | Documented sharing killer. Triggers withdrawal. Most climate content defaults here. |
| **Contentment** | No one shares a tweet that makes them feel cozy about climate. |
| **Boredom** | What weather-report voice produces. |
| **Helplessness** | Low-arousal AND demotivating. |

**Chen et al. 2023 (Communication Monographs):** On Twitter specifically, **anger > anxiety** on every measure of retweet cascade. Anger creates deeper chains because it mobilizes moral outrage and group identity.

### 1.4 Karen Nelson-Field — *Viral Marketing: The Science of Sharing* (2013)
~1,000 videos analyzed. Most rigorous video-virality study ever done.

- **High-arousal positive content shared ~70% more** than the next-highest category. Awe > anger in absolute sharing rates.
- Arousal matters more than valence (same as Berger & Milkman).
- **"Getting big is largely about getting seen."** Virality is overwhelmingly a function of reach. **Fight for one genuinely great tweet per week, not daily mediocrity.**

### 1.5 STEPPS Framework (Berger 2013, *Contagious*)
Six drivers of word-of-mouth transmission:
- **S — Social Currency:** People share what makes them look smart/in-the-know.
- **T — Triggers:** Environmental cues that prompt recall (heat waves trigger climate thoughts).
- **E — Emotion:** High-arousal only. "When we care, we share."
- **P — Public:** Things that are visible spread. Easily quotable, screenshot-able.
- **P — Practical Value:** Useful content spreads because sharing signals care.
- **S — Stories:** Narratives are Trojan horses for information.

## 2. The Information Gap — Curiosity Mechanics

### 2.1 Loewenstein 1994 — Information Gap Theory of Curiosity
*Psychological Bulletin* 116(1): 75–98.

**Core thesis:** Curiosity is an aversive deprivation state — *like hunger for information*. People are motivated to close the gap.

**The inverted-U (confirmed by Kang et al. 2009):**
- Know nothing → **no curiosity** (no gap perceived).
- Know a little → **peak curiosity** (sweet spot).
- Know almost everything → **low curiosity** (gap too small).

**For @theheat:**
- Headlines must imply the reader is one piece of information away from knowing something.
- **"Temperature in Texas hit 110°F today" contains the full answer. Nothing pulls the reader forward.**
- "A country just hit its hottest temperature ever." creates a gap (which country? how hot?).

**Upworthy caveat:** Curiosity-gap headlines worked 2012–2015 but partially decayed through overuse. Both *Nature's Scientific Reports* (2024) and Upworthy itself find descriptive headlines now outperform pure teasers. **The gap still works, but it needs to be filled by something specific and concrete, not "you won't believe what happened next."**

### 2.2 The Open Loop (Zeigarnik 1927)
Uncompleted tasks are remembered better than completed ones. Open the loop in line 1, resolve within 1–3 lines.
- "Tokyo just hit 41.8°C. / Here's what's weird: / That used to be impossible for this month."

The gap between setup and resolution is where the reader commits.

### 2.3 Reply Bait — The Productive Cousin
*Journal of Politics* finding: **anger drives information-seeking and click-through**. The structure that drives replies: **leave a curiosity gap readers want to close with their own knowledge**. A surprising climate fact without explanation creates the "wait, how?" moment.

## 3. Concrete > Abstract — How Numbers Land

### 3.1 The Rokia Study (Small, Loewenstein & Slovic 2007)
*OBHDP* 102(2): 143–153.

Subjects shown:
- (a) Rokia's story (7-year-old girl in Mali, named, photographed)
- (b) Statistical data ("more than 11 million people face hunger")
- (c) Both combined

**Results:**
- Rokia alone got **~2x the donations** of statistics alone.
- **Rokia + statistics got LESS than Rokia alone** — adding numbers suppressed emotional activation.
- Priming analytical thinking (doing math first) REDUCED donations.

**"If I look at the mass, I will never act. If I look at the one, I will."**

**For @theheat:** Climate data IS "the mass." Lead with the single identifiable thing.

### 3.2 The Concreteness Principle (Paivio dual-coding)
**Abstract numbers fail:** "3 million lbs of plastic" fails because millions is abstract, no anchor, no image, no pathos.

**Concrete comparisons succeed:** "Equivalent to a blue whale every hour" succeeds because:
- Blue whale = concrete visual
- "Every hour" = temporal concreteness, forces imagined duration
- Rate framing triggers continued accumulation
- Compares to something emotional
- Reader multiplies in their head — a participation moment

**Heath rule:** Don't use a number. Use a comparison that makes the number mean something.

**For @theheat — historical-human comparisons win over physical metaphors:**
- "Last time it was this hot in Buenos Aires, the Great Depression hadn't started" beats "hotter than a blue whale's body temperature"
- Historical records give the reader social currency (they feel cultured, not just informed)
- Physical metaphors feel like a science museum placard

### 3.3 Anchoring (Tversky & Kahneman 1974)
*Science* 185: 1124–1131.

**Wheel of Fortune:** Subjects spun wheel (10 or 65), then estimated % of African nations in UN. Anchored on 10 → ~25%. Anchored on 65 → ~45%. **Arbitrary numbers shift judgment by ~20 percentage points.**

**For @theheat:** The first number you drop becomes the anchor. Lead with the most extreme.

### 3.4 Unexpected Specificity
Brain registers a precise number as **higher-credibility** than a round number.
- "It rained for 37 days" beats "It rained for a month"
- "41.8°C" beats "very hot"
- "189 consecutive days" beats "almost a year"

Precise numbers generate both verbal and numeric mental representations, doubling encoding.

## 4. Social Currency — Why People Share to Signal

**Signaling theory (Spence 1973):** Every share is a costly signal attaching the sharer's identity to the content.

**Findings:**
- **Insider information** has the highest social currency. "I have seen this before you did" is maximally shareable.
- **Making people feel smart** is a primary driver — the reader is sharing the fact that they understood the content.
- **Specificity signals expertise.** "Tuesday was the hottest June 9 in 127 years of records for Phoenix" > "record heat in Phoenix."
- **Tribal alignment** — content that lets the sharer reaffirm group identity.

**Engineering social currency:**
- Lead with what the reader won't have seen
- Let the reader play expert ("you can see this right now in X location")
- Provide an observation, not a lecture
- Reward attentive readers with NEW information

## 5. Surprise & Novelty

### 5.1 Vosoughi, Roy, Aral 2018 — *Science* 359: 1146–1151
"The Spread of True and False News Online"

- 126,000 stories, 3M people, 4.5M tweets.
- **False news is 70% more likely to be retweeted than true news.**
- **True stories take ~6x longer to reach 1,500 people.**
- **Why?** False news is more novel. **Novelty is the master variable of diffusion.**

**For @theheat:** We can't compete with falsehood's novelty advantage through accuracy alone. We must *engineer novelty* within true content — overlooked angles, new records, comparisons never made before.

### 5.2 Optimal, Not Maximal Surprise
Shang et al. 2022: too much surprise decreases credibility.

**Sweet spot:** surprise that feels earned — "I didn't know that but now I do, and it fits what I already suspected."

**Template:** "Everyone thinks [common belief]. Actually [surprising truth]."

## 6. The Pratfall Effect (Aronson, Willerman & Floyd 1966)

**Study:** 48 subjects listened to confederate either highly competent (92%) or mediocre (30%) on quiz questions. Confederate either spilled coffee or didn't.

**Findings:**
- Competent + pratfall → **most liked.**
- Mediocre + pratfall → **least liked.**

**Mechanism:** Competence is the precondition for the pratfall bonus.

**For @theheat:** Establish competence FIRST (consistent accuracy, crisp data). ONCE established, occasional self-aware imperfection increases likability.

# PART 2 — HOW TO WRITE TWEETS THAT LAND

The craft of converting research into actual tweets people stop and share.

## 7. Headlines & Openers — The Top of the Tweet

### 7.1 Ogilvy — *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1985)
> "On the average, five times as many people read the headlines as read the body copy. Unless your headline sells your product, you have wasted 90% of your money."

**For @theheat:** The first line IS the tweet. The first line must carry the lead fact + context. Saving the zinger for line 3 wastes 90% of potential impressions.

### 7.2 Wiebe / Copyhackers — Five-Second Test
If a reader can't state what the headline promises after five seconds of exposure, kill or rewrite it.

### 7.3 The First 5–7 Words
Empirical: users scroll past in ~1.5 seconds. The first 5–7 words determine whether the rest is read.
- "Anchorage recorded 82F today." — the surprise IS the opener.
- "The temperature in Anchorage was..." — snooze.

## 8. Tweet Anatomy — Structural Patterns

### 8.1 Unexpected Specificity
Precise numbers > round numbers. (See 3.4.)

### 8.2 The Negation Flip
**"Not X. Actually Y."** or **"X. But Y."**
- "It's not just hot. It's the hottest week in recorded history."
- "Not one city. Twelve."
- "The heat wave didn't break a record. It broke three."

The negation activates expectation, then violates it.

### 8.3 The Rule of Three with Rising Stakes
- "Faster. Hotter. Deadlier."
- "In 1910, never. In 1990, once. In 2025, monthly."
- "1990: X / 2005: Y / 2025: Z" — escalation requires no commentary.

### 8.4 The Twist
- "This is a heat map of Europe. (Normal.) It's from February. (Not normal.)"

### 8.5 The "Stop and Think" Hook
Questions activate **generative processing** — reader mentally attempts an answer first.
- "When was the last time it was this hot in Paris? Answer: never."

But avoid generic questions ("what do you think?") — those are engagement-farming.

### 8.6 One-Line vs Two-Line vs Three-Line
- **One-line tweets** — highest virality per word. Best for aphorism, shock number.
- **Two-line tweets** — open-loop setup + resolution. Strong for data.
- **Three-line tweets** — setup + build + punch. Strong for narrative.

## 9. Copywriting Canon

### 9.1 Schwartz — *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966)
**Five Stages of Market Sophistication:**
1. First to market — simple claim works
2. Competitors enter — bigger claims
3. Saturation — mechanism differentiator
4. More saturation — mechanism elaboration
5. Total fatigue — identification > claim

**Climate Twitter is at Stage 4–5.** Nobody believes a new "the planet is warming" claim. The wedge isn't the claim — it's specific evidence, novel mechanism, or **identity resonance**.

Schwartz's principle: **"Channel existing desires; do not try to create them."** @theheat's existing desire = the reader's suspicion that something is deeply wrong. The job is to supply the proof.

### 9.2 Cialdini — *Influence* (1984, rev. 2021)
Seven principles: **Reciprocity, Commitment & Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity, Unity** (added 2016).

**For @theheat:**
- **Scarcity** — natural climate lever (species lost, ice lost, days below freezing lost)
- **Authority** — clean data + sourcing
- **Unity** ("people who can see what's happening") — strongest and least-used

### 9.3 PAS over AIDA
**PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solution)** outperforms AIDA for short-form because it starts in pain.
- Problem (a record)
- Agitate (specifically what's unusual)
- Solution (the unspoken prompt: look, and keep looking)

### 9.4 Bly's 8 Headlines
Direct, Indirect, News, How-to, Question, Command, Reason-why, Testimonial.
**4 S formula** for body copy: Simple, Short, Sincere, Specific.

## 10. Voice & Tone

### 10.1 Heath Brothers — SUCCESs
Six principles:
- **Simple** — core message stripped to essentials
- **Unexpected** — violate schemas (CSPI popcorn = more saturated fat than Big Mac + steak combined)
- **Concrete** — sensory detail
- **Credible** — anti-authorities beat authorities; vivid detail > statistics
- **Emotional** — Rokia study
- **Stories** — narrative as Trojan horse

### 10.2 Show, Don't Tell
- Never add meta-commentary ("THIS IS SERIOUS," "HURRICANE-FORCE," "EXTREME force")
- If you have to tell the reader to be impressed, you failed
- Let the data land

### 10.3 Loss Framing > Gain Framing (Kahneman)
Losses loom ~2× as large as gains.
**For @theheat:** "You could lose this" beats "you could gain this." Loss-frame: what's **going away**, what's **being broken**, what **will not happen again**.

### 10.4 Peak-End Rule (Kahneman et al. 1993)
People judge experiences by the **peak intensity** and the **ending**, not the integral.

**For @theheat:** A tweet needs a peak moment (the number that hits) and a strong ending. Don't end with "but it's complicated" or trailing reservations. End on the punch.

## 11. The Power of Memes

### 11.1 Dawkins (1976) — The Transmissible Kernel
A meme is "a unit of cultural transmission" — a replicator. The tweet is not the unit; the **transmissible kernel** inside it is (a number, a record, a phrasing). Design tweets so the kernel can be ripped out and retold.

### 11.2 Blackmore (1999) — Three Criteria
- **Fidelity** — copied accurately
- **Fecundity** — produces many copies
- **Longevity** — persists over time

A @theheat tweet must be easy to retype by hand, easy to attach to a chart, and stay true longer than the news cycle.

### 11.3 Shifman (2014) — Form, Content, Stance
Six features of highly spreadable content: positivity-tinged humor, simplicity, repetitiveness, incongruous juxtaposition, whimsical content, and a **provocative/participatory hook**.

**Conclusion:** Memes with a hook that invites participation outperform "closed" content. **Give people a socket to plug into.**

### 11.4 The Format Mechanic
Verbal component has greater remix capacity than visual. The visual anchors recognition; the text is the variable.

**For @theheat:** A house visual template (specific chart style, specific card layout) turns every tweet into a recognizable meme format. **The text inside is the variable that carries the day's payload.**

# PART 3 — DEEPER AUDIENCE PSYCHOLOGY

The research on what's actually happening in readers' brains.

## 12. Negativity Bias

### 12.1 Rozin & Royzman 2001
*Personality and Social Psychology Review* 5: 296–320.

Four manifestations:
1. **Negative potency** — negatives are stronger than equivalent positives
2. **Steeper negative gradients** — negativity grows more rapidly with proximity
3. **Negativity dominance** — when positive + negative combine, result is more negative
4. **Negative differentiation** — negatives have richer mental representations

### 12.2 Soroka, Fournier & Nir 2019 (PNAS)
17-country, 6-continent psychophysiological study. **Across all cultures, humans show negativity bias in physiological arousal when watching news.** Species-level, not cultural.

### 12.3 Why Negative Beats Positive (Even When People SAY They Prefer Positive)
- **Robertson et al. 2023** (Nature Human Behaviour): 23,000 Upworthy A/B-tested headlines. Each negative word **raises CTR by 2.3%**, each positive word **lowers it by 1.0%**.
- **Scientific Reports 2024:** 95,282 articles, 579M social posts. Negative news **1.91× more likely to be shared**. With retweets: **34%–61% increase in shares**.
- **Cambridge Judge 2024:** Negativity is shared more between weak ties; positivity between close ties. **Twitter's default network is weak-tie, so the platform structurally selects for negative content.**

## 13. Moral Contagion & Out-Group Animosity

### 13.1 Brady et al. 2017 (PNAS) — Moral Contagion
**Each additional moral-emotional word in a tweet increases diffusion by ~20%.** Effect within-group only — moral language travels *inside* ideological networks.

A 2024 meta-analysis replicated this at ~17% per word.

### 13.2 The MAD Model (Brady, Crockett, Van Bavel 2020)
Three drivers:
- **Motivation** — self-presentation, group membership
- **Attention** — moral content captures attention preferentially
- **Design** — platform features amplify moral content

### 13.3 Rathje et al. 2021 (PNAS) — Out-Group Animosity
2.7M posts. **Each additional out-group word raised share probability by 35%–57%.** Out-group language was the **single strongest predictor of sharing** — stronger than in-group language, moral-emotional language, or negative emotion.

**For @theheat:** Naming an antagonist is a powerful share lever. BUT — comes with brand cost. Safe path: frame "inaction," "the status quo," or industries identified by data (factually) as antagonist, without partisan invective.

## 14. Cognitive Biases That Drive Sharing

### 14.1 Availability Heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman 1973)
People estimate probability by how easily examples come to mind. Vivid, recent, emotional events feel more probable than their actual frequencies.

**For @theheat:** A tweet that vividly renders a single extreme event updates the reader's internal frequency estimate of climate events far more than aggregate statistics do.

### 14.2 Mere Exposure Effect (Zajonc 1968)
Repeated exposure to a neutral stimulus increases liking. Bornstein 1989 meta-analysis (208 experiments): effect size r = 0.26, peaks at 10–20 exposures.

**For @theheat:** Daily rhythm builds preference independent of content quality. Miss two weeks → mere-exposure accumulation resets.

### 14.3 Von Restorff / Isolation Effect (1933)
A distinctive item in a list is remembered better than its neighbors.

**For @theheat:** Whatever makes a tweet visually or structurally distinct in the feed (chart style, card format, voice cadence) compounds recall.

### 14.4 Status Quo Bias and Loss Framing (Kahneman)
Losses loom ~2× as large as gains. Bar-Hillel & Neter 1996: status quo is broken by clear downside framing.

## 15. Identity & Tribes

### 15.1 Costly Signaling (Spence, Zahavi)
Signals are trustworthy when they're expensive. On social media, the "expense" is reputational — sharing something that alienates half your audience proves conviction.

### 15.2 Strategic Identity Signaling (Smaldino & Pérez 2022, PNAS)
Individuals **encrypt** identity signals when audience is politically diverse — humor, references, fashion as covert in-group markers. Insiders recognize; outsiders don't flag.

**For @theheat:** A house visual style is literally a costly signal — proving editorial investment, identifying the account to in-group consumers.

### 15.3 In-Group Effects
ScienceDirect 2024: positive in-group effects on participation. Group members more likely to use social media when group identity is affirmed.

**Climate-literate readers share @theheat partly because sharing affirms identity as "people who take this seriously."**

## 16. Climate-Specific Audience Research

### 16.1 Pew Research 2021
- **45% of Gen Z and 40% of Millennials** interact with climate content monthly vs. 27% of Boomers.
- **32% of Gen Z** have taken climate action vs. 21% of Boomers.
- **Gen Z is the core sharing audience.**

### 16.2 Gen Z Emotional Profile
- 40–70% report dread/sadness about climate.
- **Determination (not anger) is the strongest predictor of Gen Z climate action.** Approach-oriented emotions > avoidance-oriented emotions.

### 16.3 Doom Fatigue Is Real
- **>80% of climate news uses disaster/doom frame** (Oxford Institute of Journalism).
- **3 consecutive days of doom exposure produces more fear, less hope, MORE disengagement.**
- **Hope is the single most consistent predictor** of political engagement, policy support, and conservation behavior.
- Solutions journalism increases positive affect WITHOUT sacrificing urgency.
- Crisis framing wins initial sharing. Opportunity framing wins sustained engagement and memory.
- **Ettinger et al. 2021** (*Climatic Change*): hope frames produced higher sustained behavior change than doom frames.
- **Falkenberg et al. 2022** (*Nature Climate Change*): climate contrarian content grew **16x in retweet engagement 2015–2021** vs. **4x for pro-climate content**.

**For @theheat:** Pure doom is underperforming. Playbook: **awe + anger at specific actors + occasional wins/solutions, anchored in concrete local or human detail, with novelty.** Sadness should be rare; when used, pair with high-arousal emotion.

# PART 4 — PROOF POINTS

What's actually gone viral in the world we're operating in.

## 17. Climate Viral Case Studies

### 17.1 Greta Thunberg — "How Dare You" (Sept 2019)
The phrase trended globally within 72 hours. Templated into image macros across YouTube and Twitter.

Mechanics:
- **Negation with moral-emotional weight** — "how dare you" is pure outrage in three words
- **Repetition** — spoken as a refrain, templates for re-quotation
- **Pratfall generator** — adults mocking a 16-year-old triggered counter-amplification
- **Identity activation** — split audiences for/against, generating out-group sharing

Converted a generational grievance into a portable phrase carrying the full emotional payload.

### 17.2 Warming Stripes (Ed Hawkins, 2018)
Most successful single climate visualization of the 2010s–2020s:
- 1M+ downloads in week 1
- Used on murals, T-shirts, climate strike posters, *The Economist* cover (2019)
- Hawkins received an MBE in 2020 for science communication

Why it works (Shifman decomposition):
- **Form:** Fixed grammar (stripes, blue→red, chronological) infinitely re-deployable
- **Content:** Zero text, zero chart chrome — visual is the argument
- **Stance:** Scientific authorial restraint

**The single most instructive meme for @theheat's own chart system. A house visual template with zero chrome that admits infinite payload substitution.**

### 17.3 Hockey Stick Graph (Mann et al. 1999)
The *shape* (flat handle, dramatic blade) **IS** the argument, parseable in under one second.

### 17.4 What Viral Climate Charts Share
- **One variable** — temperature, ice, CO₂. Not multi-variable dashboards.
- **One time axis** — no zoom switcher. Reader sees full span.
- **A dramatic shape** — line that hockey-sticks, stripes that redshift.
- **Zero chart chrome when possible** — no axis labels, no gridlines.
- **One color grammar** — red means worse, always.
- **Regional derivative variants** — meta-chart spawns city/state/country variants.

### 17.5 Bravo, Silva Luna, Walter 2025 (Journalism Studies)
Viral climate imagery analysis:
- **Maps outperform charts** for regional resonance.
- **Before/after photos** outperform abstract visualization.
- **People in images** increase sharing vs pure landscape.
- **Iconic species** (polar bears, coral, koalas) still work despite "cliché" critique.

## 18. Twitter Account Studies

### 18.1 Naval Ravikant
Aphoristic compression + contrast structure. **Three-part taxonomies. "Not X. Y." negation flips. Single-thought tweets** — one idea, no caveats. **Portability** — works on a mug, a Notion page, a screenshot.

### 18.2 Visakan Veerasamy (@visakanv)
- "Dumb tweeting to precipitate smart writing" — post low-stakes to find what resonates
- Twitter as a second brain — every thread references older threads
- "Be purpose-driven. Know your end-goal."

### 18.3 Paul Graham — "Founder Mode" (2024)
Went viral because it **named a latent feeling**. Schwartz Stage-5 work: market saturated with generic startup advice. Graham coined a label that splits insiders from outsiders. **Language that pre-activates identity.**

### 18.4 NASA & Astro Accounts
- Carina Nebula: NASA's all-time most-engaged social post (>7M engagements).
- The image is the product. Text annotates.
- **Institutional reticence earns trust; one striking image does the work.**

### 18.5 @extremetemps — The Model
Run by a climatologist (mherrera.org/temp.htm).
- **Near-identical card format**: flag emoji + country + station + value + context
- **No commentary** — the data is the whole tweet
- Heavy use of **records broken** as the unit of news
- **Always the same visual grammar**

Textbook **Shifman meme format**: fixed template (form) + varying payload (content) + stable stance (deadpan record-keeping). Every post reinforces the template, increasing recognition velocity in the feed.

# PART 5 — ANTI-PATTERNS (What KILLS Virality)

The kill list. Avoiding these matters more than any positive technique.

## 19. The Fatal Patterns

1. **Weather-report voice** — "Temperatures in the Southwest reached…". No arousal. No narrator. No personality.
2. **Label:value syntax** — "Phoenix: 118°F. Record: 122°F (2017)." Data-dump triggers analytical mode, which **kills emotional activation** (Rokia/sympathy-callousness effect).
3. **Hedges** — "according to," "experts warn," "a new study shows." Drains urgency.
4. **Date repetition** — "Yesterday, 7/14, Tuesday, July 14, 2024…" wastes character count.
5. **Closing the curiosity gap immediately** — giving the full answer in the same breath as the question.
6. **Explaining what things mean** — converts the reader from discoverer to student. Students don't share.
7. **Tier explainers** — "Category 4 means winds over 130 mph." Once you're explaining definitions, social-currency-by-expertise is gone.
8. **Sadness without anger or awe** — Berger & Milkman's documented sharing killer.
9. **Preachy tone / moralizing** — robs the reader of the opportunity to react themselves.
10. **Abstract statistics without anchor** — "3 million lbs of plastic."
11. **Zero stance** — pure-neutral reporting triggers no dominance emotion.
12. **Too-familiar facts** — violates Loewenstein's inverted-U. If the reader could write this tweet, they have nothing to gain by sharing it.
13. **Buried lede** — the first 5–7 words are everything.
14. **Meta-commentary** — "THIS IS SERIOUS," "HURRICANE-FORCE," "EXTREME force," "catastrophic," "life-threatening." Tell-don't-show failure.
15. **Bureaucratic noise** — "-26" suffixes, agency names as openers ("NWS issued"), GDACS jargon nobody knows.

## 20. Twitter/X Specific (algorithm)

The core recommendation code was open-sourced March 31, 2023 (github.com/twitter/the-algorithm).

### 20.1 Engagement weights
- **Reply + author engages back** — ~150× a like (the single highest-weighted signal)
- **Retweet** — ~20× a like
- **Bookmark** — ~10× a like
- **Like** — 1× baseline

The algorithm prices conversation far above passive approval.

### 20.2 Engagement velocity
**The first 30–60 minutes determine whether the For You feed distributes the post or lets it die.** A tweet getting 5 engagements in 10 minutes reaches 10–100× more than the same engagement spread over 24 hours.

### 20.3 Dwell time
Algorithm tracks dwell time on tweet detail (15+ seconds threshold) and profile view (20+ seconds). **This favors data + chart content** that makes readers stop and look.

### 20.4 What the algorithm actively suppresses
- **External links from non-Premium accounts** — median engagement effectively zero. 30–50% reach penalty.
- **More than 2–3 hashtags** — flagged as spam.
- **Engagement farming** — "Like if you agree" style downweighted.
- **@-mention stuffing** — deboosted similarly to links.
- **10+ tweets in 5 minutes** — spam pattern.

### 20.5 Twitter specifics, broader benchmarks
- Median engagement rate: ~0.035%. A 1% rate beats 95% of the platform.
- 71–100 characters generate ~17% more engagement than shorter tweets.
- Long-form posts (4000-char) now favored over multi-tweet threads (Musk Oct 2023).

## 21. Visual Content Multipliers

- **AdWeek 2014:** +150% retweets, +89% favorites, +18% clicks with image
- **AJNR 2021 academic study:** image presence increased engagement by **28.75×**
- Lower-authority accounts: 5–9× retweets, 4–12× favorites with images

**Maps vs charts vs photos:**
- Maps outperform charts for regional resonance
- Before/after photos viralize consistently (zero cognitive load)
- GIFs / silent looping video are dwell-time winners (auto-play)

## 22. Operational Levers

(Use these to amplify already-great content. Never to rescue mediocre content.)

1. **House visual template** — Hawkins Warming Stripes lesson. Fixed card grammar = recognizable Heat-brand meme format.
2. **Reply to your own tweet** with second-beat fact — algorithm's richest signal (~150× a like).
3. **Aim for bookmarkable content** — records, data references, regional stats are bookmark-bait (10× weighted).
4. **Premium verification** — documented 4×/2× algorithmic boost.
5. **Post at engagement-velocity windows** — first 30–60 minutes determine reach.
6. **Mere-exposure rhythm** — daily cadence builds preference. Missing 2 weeks resets accumulation.
7. **Reply-to-self for sources** instead of in-tweet links (which are suppressed).

## Integrated Playbook for @theheat

**The order of operations:**

1. **The "wait, what?" test.** Say the tweet out loud. If a smart non-expert wouldn't stop and say "wait, what?" — kill it. If the response is "yeah, the planet is warming, I know" — rewrite.

2. **The love-it test.** Do we chuckle? Want to send it to a friend? Think it's astounding? If not — kill it. Frequency is not your friend; arousal-per-post is.

3. **Optimize for arousal, not accuracy.** Accuracy is the floor, not the ceiling. Every tweet must pass: **"what physical state does this trigger in the reader?"** If "mild sadness" or "neutral informed" — kill it.

4. **The four-emotion shortlist.** Lead with **awe, anger, anxiety, or amusement**. Awe + specificity (scale-shock) is the bonus track.

5. **The anchor rule.** First 5–7 words carry the whole tweet. A number, a shocking verb, a specific place. Never "Today," "Yesterday," "Recently," "According to."

6. **Concrete > abstract, always.** Historical-human comparisons beat physical metaphors. "Last time it was this hot, the stock market hadn't crashed" > "hotter than X."

7. **Leave the gap open.** State the shocking fact. Don't explain the mechanism. Don't interpret. Let the reader fill it in.

8. **Identifiable > statistical.** One farmer in one county. One glacier. One bird. One day. Not aggregates first.

9. **Novelty engineering.** Every tweet must contain *something new*. New record, comparison, juxtaposition, rate, local angle.

10. **Social currency fit.** Does sharing this make the reader look (a) informed, (b) caring, (c) righteous, or (d) witty? If none, kill it.

11. **Establish competence, then use pratfall sparingly.** Consistent accuracy first. Then occasional humanized moments.

12. **The Gen Z emotional frame.** Determination > anger > sadness. Approach-oriented. Hope + awe + urgency beats pure crisis framing.

13. **Voice rules (bans):**
    - No "experts warn"
    - No "according to"
    - No "this is a wake-up call"
    - No label:value date strings
    - No explaining what tier/category means
    - No explaining why the reader should care. **Trust them.**

14. **The editorial bar.** Tweet must be (awe-inducing OR anger-inducing OR anxiety-inducing OR laugh-inducing) **AND** contain a novel concrete fact **AND** give the reader social currency to share.

15. **Hit the bar or skip the day.** Quality over volume.

16. **Then — and only then — apply amplification tactics.** A great tweet plus strong distribution beats a great tweet alone. A mediocre tweet plus amplification is just amplified mediocrity.
